"Those who forget the past are condemned to repeat it." Although the United States is known for celebrating freedom, human rights, and diversity, those essential values have been challenged in times of crisis. Even before the Second World War, immigrants from China and Japan faced discrimination in the U.S. When Pearl Harbor was bombed in December of 1941, the situation got much worse. About 110,000 Americans of Japanese descent, even if they had never lived in Japan, were treated as the enemy and forced to live in "relocation camps" for years.

Not one was ever charged with a crime. The internment camps were a long time ago, but there are still people alive today who were in them. And many Americans can tell us about experiences their parents or grandparents had in the camps. For this project, you will begin with several group activities to help you and your peers become familiar with this time period. What was it like for a Japanese American during World War II? Had anything like this happened before in our country?

Has anything similar happened in more recent history? Your challenge will be to report on this dark period of American history through research and interviews. You will produce a compelling video podcast using interviews, narration, and still photographs to help others remember what happened in the past, and to ensure that it is never repeated.
